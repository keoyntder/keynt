Earth is the third planet from the Sun and the only known planet capable of sustaining life. It has a diverse structure, consisting of a solid crust, a molten mantle, and a dense core, with about 71% of its surface covered by water. Surrounded by an atmosphere rich in oxygen and nitrogen, it supports a wide variety of ecosystems and organisms. Earth orbits the Sun at an average distance of 93 million miles, completing a rotation every 24 hours and a revolution every 365.25 days.

Jupiter is the fifth planet from the Sun and the largest in our solar system, known for its massive size and striking appearance. It is a gas giant primarily composed of hydrogen and helium, with no solid surface and a turbulent atmosphere marked by colorful bands and the iconic Great Red Spot. Jupiter has a strong magnetic field and at least 95 moons, including the four largest, known as the Galilean moons: Io, Europa, Ganymede, and Callisto. Orbiting the Sun at an average distance of 484 million miles, it completes a rotation in just 10 hours but takes nearly 12 Earth years to complete one orbit.

Mars is the fourth planet from the Sun in our solar system and is often called the "Red Planet" due to its reddish appearance, caused by iron oxide (rust) on its surface. It is a terrestrial planet with a thin atmosphere, featuring surface features like valleys, mountains, and the largest volcano in the solar system, Olympus Mons. Mars is a primary focus of scientific exploration because it may have once had liquid water and conditions suitable for life.

Saturn is the sixth planet from the Sun and the second largest in our solar system, known for its distinctive ring system made up of ice particles, dust, and rock. It is a gas giant primarily composed of hydrogen and helium, with a small rocky core. Saturn has at least 82 moons, including the largest, Titan, and is orbited by thousands of ringlets that extend outwards for thousands of miles. It orbits the Sun at an average distance of 886 million miles, completing a rotation in about 10.7 hours and a revolution every 29.5 Earth years.

Mercury is the smallest and innermost planet in our solar system, known for its extreme temperatures and lack of atmosphere. It is a rocky planet with a heavily cratered surface and a metallic core, and it orbits the Sun at an average distance of 36 million miles. Mercury has a slow rotation, completing one day in about 59 Earth days, but a fast revolution, completing one orbit in just 88 Earth days.

Neptune is the eighth and farthest planet from the Sun in our solar system, known for its striking blue color and powerful winds. It is a gas giant primarily composed of hydrogen, helium, and methane, with a small rocky core. Neptune has at least 14 moons, including the largest, Triton, and a faint ring system. It orbits the Sun at an average distance of 2.8 billion miles, completing a rotation in about 16 hours and a revolution every 165 Earth years.
